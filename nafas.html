The Sun is a massive, glowing ball of hot gases at the center of our solar system, primarily composed of hydrogen and helium. It is a G-type main-sequence star, also known as a yellow dwarf, and has a diameter of about 1.39 million kilometers (864,000 miles). The Sun’s core is the powerhouse where nuclear fusion occurs, converting hydrogen into helium and releasing immense energy in the form of light and heat. This energy sustains life on Earth, drives weather patterns, and fuels photosynthesis in plants. The Sun has several layers, including the core, radiative zone, convective zone, photosphere, chromosphere, and corona. It follows an 11-year solar cycle characterized by variations in solar activity such as sunspots and solar flares. The Sun’s immense gravitational pull keeps the planets, asteroids, comets, and other objects in our solar system in orbit around it. Despite being an average-sized star, the Sun accounts for over 99.8% of the total mass of the solar system.

The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite and the fifth-largest moon in the solar system. It orbits Earth at an average distance of about 384,400 kilometers (238,855 miles) and takes approximately 27.3 days to complete one orbit. The Moon's surface is characterized by craters, mountains, and vast plains of basaltic lava known as "maria." It lacks an atmosphere, which means it has no weather and extreme temperature variations between day and night. The Moon’s gravitational pull affects Earth's tides, creating the ebb and flow of ocean waters. The Moon also plays a crucial role in stabilizing Earth's axial tilt, which helps maintain a relatively stable climate. Its phases, from new moon to full moon, result from the varying amounts of sunlight it reflects as it orbits Earth.
